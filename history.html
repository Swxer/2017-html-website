    • Brief History Of The Network

    Over the years, the network has been rennovated and innovated simultaneously, until it becomes what it is today. However, in order to understand what makes the network as it is today, the following timeline will break it down one by one on how the network was developed.

    1957 – USSR launches Sputnik into space. In response, the US creates the Advanced Research Projects Agency (ARPA) with the mission of becoming the leading force in science and new technologies. This is basically the very first wireless machine that has been used to test the strength of the connection between outer space and earth. (An image below is a Sputnik, a form of satellite.)

    1985 - The FCC release three segments of the unlicensed frequency spectrum to the public. Those three unlicensed frequency were at 9000MHz, 2.4GHz and 5GHz.

    1988 - NCR, orginally known as National Cash Register Company, wanted to connect wireless cash registers and submits a request to the Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers (IEEE), which sets up a committe called "802.11" to help define a standard for wireless technology.

    1997 - The 802.11 standard is established, using the 900MHz band.

    1999 - The term "Wi-Fi" becomes a registered trademark of the Wi-Fi Alliance, a global non-profit organization that promotes wireless technology and standardisation. Wi-Fi refers to a wireless network product that conforms to one of the 802.11 standards.

    2001 - D-Link releases its first 802.11b access point in retail locations, kicking off a revolution of wireless networking in homes and eventually, business.

    2004 - The first Wi-Fi certified Personal Digital Assistant (PDA) and cell phones reach the market, including the HP iPAQ PocketPC, the Nokia 9500 COmmunicator and the Motorola MPx

    2005 - The word "Wi-Fi is added to the Merriam-Webster English Dictionary. The first Wi-fi gaming device and digital still camera becomes available.

    2006 - D-Link releases 802.11g and 802.11n wireless adapters and routers. The 802.11n standard is ratified, optimised at 5GHz, although multi-channel technology lets it operate at 2.4GHz aswell.

    2010 - The number of global Wi-Fi hotspots exceeds 1 million with the help of Starbucks, which introduces free Wi-Fi at all of its company-operated U.S. locations. D-Link releases its first 802.11n wireless network camera, the DCS-930L.

    2013 - D-Link releases a full line of 802.11ac wireless adapters and routers.
